# YouTube Script Writing Skill

Expert YouTube script writing optimized for retention, engagement, and conversion. Based on techniques from top AI/tech creators (Fireship, MrBeast, Marques Brownlee) and retention science.

## When to Use
- Writing scripts for YouTube videos (especially tech/AI demos)
- Rewriting existing scripts for better retention
- Reviewing scripts for pacing and engagement issues

## Core Principles

### 1. The Hook (First 5 Seconds)
The viewer decides to stay or leave in the first 5 seconds. No exceptions.

**What works:**
- Bold, contrarian statement ("Here's what's wrong with every AI you've used")
- A visual payoff preview ("Watch what happens when I argue with my AI")
- Pattern interrupt (unexpected visual, sound, or statement)
- Problem the viewer recognizes instantly

**What kills retention:**
- "Hey guys, welcome to my channel" (generic intro)
- Channel name, subscribe request, or sponsor up front
- Explaining who you are before showing why they should care
- Throat-clearing ("So today I wanted to talk about...")

**Formula:** CLAIM + PROOF PREVIEW in under 10 seconds.

### 2. Structure: Demo Before Theory

Never explain how something works before showing what it does. The viewer needs to want the explanation before you give it.

**Pattern:**
1. Show the impressive result (5-15 sec)
2. Create curiosity gap ("How does this work?")
3. Now explain the mechanism
4. Show it again with understanding

**Fireship's approach:** 100 seconds of pure demonstration density. Every sentence either shows something or sets up the next thing shown.

### 3. Pattern Interrupts (Every 60-90 Seconds)

Viewer attention decays on a curve. Reset it with pattern interrupts:

- **Visual change**: New camera angle, screen recording, diagram, animation
- **Tonal shift**: Serious to casual, explanation to joke, talking head to demo
- **New section**: Clear topic pivot with visual marker
- **Question**: Ask the viewer something (rhetorical counts)
- **Contrast**: "Most people think X. But actually Y."

**Rule:** If 90 seconds pass without a visual or tonal change, you're losing viewers.

### 4. Pacing and Cuts

**Cut everything that isn't earning its seconds:**
- Remove filler words ("um", "so basically", "you know")
- Cut pauses longer than 0.5 seconds
- Remove repeated information (say it once, well)
- Kill throat-clearing sentences ("Let me explain what I mean by that")
- If a sentence doesn't add new information or tension, delete it

**Sentence rhythm:** Vary length. Short punchy sentences for impact. Longer ones for flow. Never three long sentences in a row.

**Fireship density:** ~3-4 ideas per minute. Every sentence should either teach, show, or create tension.

### 5. The Curiosity Gap

Keep viewers watching by always having an open loop:

- "But here's where it gets interesting..."
- "That's not even the best part."
- Show a result before explaining the method
- Preview what's coming ("In a minute I'll show you X, but first...")

**Never close all loops at once.** Always have the next thing teased before finishing the current thing.

### 6. Ending

**End abruptly.** The worst thing you can do is a long wind-down.

- No "thanks for watching"
- No "don't forget to like and subscribe"
- No summary of what they just saw
- End on the strongest statement or most impressive moment
- Or end with a forward tease for the next video

**MrBeast principle:** The video ends when the content ends. Not one second after.

### 7. Script Formatting

Write scripts in spoken language, not written language:

- Contractions always ("it's" not "it is", "don't" not "do not")
- Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences max)
- Mark visual cues: `[SCREEN: demo of X]` or `[CUT TO: close-up]`
- Mark tone shifts: `[beat]` for pause, `[faster]` for pace change
- Write how you talk, then tighten

### 8. Authenticity Over Polish

Viewers can smell scripted. The best scripts sound unscripted:

- Keep genuine reactions and personality
- Imperfect delivery > robotic perfection
- Real opinions > safe statements
- Arguing with your own product > praising it

### 9. The "So What?" Test

Every section must pass the viewer's internal "so what?" filter:

- Feature described? So what does it DO for me?
- Technology explained? So what does it MEAN?
- Demo shown? So what makes this DIFFERENT?

Always connect back to what the viewer cares about.

## Script Review Checklist

When reviewing a script, check:

- [ ] Hook in first 5 seconds (no preamble)
- [ ] Demo/result shown before explanation
- [ ] Pattern interrupt every 60-90 seconds
- [ ] No filler sentences (every line earns its place)
- [ ] Open loops maintained (curiosity gaps)
- [ ] Varied sentence rhythm
- [ ] Written in spoken language
- [ ] Ends abruptly on a strong note
- [ ] Passes "so what?" test for every section
- [ ] Authentic voice (not corporate/salesy)

## Script Template

```
[HOOK - 5 seconds]
Bold claim or visual payoff preview.

[DEMO - 15-30 seconds]
Show the impressive thing. No explanation yet.

[CURIOSITY GAP]
"Here's why this works..."

[MECHANISM - 60-90 seconds]
Explain the key concept. One concept only.
Use analogy. Show visually.

[PATTERN INTERRUPT]
Shift to live demo / new angle / tonal change.

[LIVE DEMO - 2-3 minutes]
Real usage. Real reactions. Real argument.
Let the product speak.

[PAYOFF]
Connect back to the hook claim. Prove it.

[FORWARD TEASE]
"Next time: [exciting thing]"

[END - abrupt]
```

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **The Wikipedia intro**: "AI, or artificial intelligence, is a field of computer science..."
- **The feature list**: "It can do X, Y, Z, and also A, B, C..."
- **The humble brag**: "We've been working really hard on this..."
- **The apology**: "I know the UI isn't perfect yet but..."
- **The long goodbye**: "Thanks for watching, please like, subscribe, hit the bell..."
- **The recap**: "So as we saw today, AIDEN can do..." (they just saw it)

## Tone for Tech/AI Demos

- Confident but not arrogant
- Opinionated ("here's what's wrong" > "some people think")
- Conversational (talking to one smart friend, not an audience)
- Fast-paced but not breathless
- Show don't tell (demo > explanation)

## Arguments

When called with arguments, treat them as either:
1. A script to rewrite using these principles
2. A topic/product to write a script about
3. A specific aspect to focus on (e.g., "hook only", "review this script")
